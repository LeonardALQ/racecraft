Part I

The Model

Before any technique, three things govern everything that follows: the tiny contact patch that is your only link to the road, the finite grip it provides, and where you point your eyes. Get the model right and every input later becomes obvious.

Section 01Draft

The Tyre & Contact Patch

  • Everything you ask the bike to do reaches the road through two contact patches, each roughly the size of your palm. That patch is your entire grip budget — there is nothing else.
  • Grip is not fixed. It rises and falls with the load on the tyre, its temperature, and the surface. Cold tyres grip far less, which is why you bring them up to temperature before you push.
  • The patch is largest when the bike is upright. As you lean, you ride onto the rounded shoulder of the tyre and the patch shrinks — which is exactly why lean angle costs you grip you could otherwise spend on braking or drive.
Section 02

The Traction Budget

A tyre has one budget of grip, shared between slowing or driving (longitudinal) and turning (lateral).11Longitudinal grip is what you spend on brake and throttle; lateral grip is what you spend on lean. One budget feeds both. Spend it all in one direction and there is none left for the other.

Section 03Draft

Vision & Where to Look

  • You go where you look. The bike follows your eyes. Fixate on the thing that scares you and you will steer straight into it — that's target fixation.
  • Keep your eyes up and look through the corner to where you want to be next: turn-in, then apex, then exit. Your peripheral vision handles the rest.
  • This is the antidote to running wide. The rider who runs wide and crashes is usually staring at the edge of the road. Lift your eyes to the exit and the bike comes back to you — precisely when you're tempted to panic and dump the throttle (§06).
Part II

The Inputs

Every control — bars, brakes, throttle, gears, and your own body — spends from the single grip budget. Used in the right order and proportion they hand off to one another seamlessly. Used carelessly they overspend, and the tyre lets go.

Section 04Draft

Counter-steering

  • Above walking pace you don't lean the bike by leaning your body — you steer the bars momentarily the "wrong" way. Push the inside bar (push the right bar to go right) and the bike falls into the turn.
  • This briefly moves the contact patches out from under the centre of mass, so the bike rolls into lean. To stand it back up, you ease or reverse the pressure.
  • It's the only way lean is initiated — whether you think about it or not. Everything in Making the Bike Lean Quickly (§10) is about how fast the bike answers this one input.
Section 05Expanded

Braking & Trail-braking

  • Brake in a pattern of 5% → 100%: squeeze in lightly first to load the front tyre and let it take the weight, then build to peak pressure. Never grab it — an unloaded front locks or washes.
  • Trail-braking is carrying that brake past the turn-in point and bleeding it off as you add lean. You're trading one input for another along the grip budget: as lean rises, brake falls, and the sum stays near the limit (this is the curved path across Fig. 2.1).
  • Done right there is no coasting moment between braking and cornering — the front stays loaded and the bike keeps turning all the way to the apex.
Section 06

Throttle

  • Throttle must be applied smoothly, evenly, and consistently. This does not mean you take multiple seconds to apply it — it means you actively roll the throttle on and off proportional to your other inputs (mainly lean and braking). You should never dump the throttle.
  • Dumping the throttle compresses the front forks significantly, and is one of the most common reasons people crash when they run wide. They get scared, they dump the throttle, they're already braking or leaning hard and don't have much travel left. The moment they let go, they run out of travel and crash.
  • If you are running wide, do not dump the throttle. Hold steady — this lets you decelerate and also lets you turn more.
Section 07

Gear Changes

  • A gear change is another input you should not be manipulating near maximal lean angle.
  • Downshift during maximal braking; upshift as you exit the corner.
Section 08

Leaning

There are two different angles we care about:

  • Chassis lean angle — the angle of the bike relative to the ground. The steeper this angle, the smaller the contact patch, and therefore the less speed you can carry.
  • System lean angle — the angle from the tyre's contact patch to the centre of mass (bike + rider combined).

To maintain more speed we want to minimise chassis lean. The steeper that angle, the smaller the contact patch — which effectively "gears the bike down," exactly the way gears work.

To minimise chassis lean, we offset the centre of gravity of the system — i.e. maximise the system lean angle while keeping the chassis more upright. (How we do that is Body Position, §09.)

Section 09Draft

Body Position

  • Body position is how you cheat lean angle. By hanging your body to the inside you move the system's centre of mass inboard, so the bike makes the same cornering force at a smaller chassis lean angle — keeping a bigger contact patch (§08).
  • Get your weight off the seat and to the inside: inside cheek off the seat, head and shoulder down and into the corner, weight on the inside peg.
  • Support yourself with your legs and core, not your arms. A loose grip on the bars is essential — tension feeds straight into the steering and ruins the smooth throttle and brake inputs everything else depends on.

Section 10

Making the Bike Lean Quickly

To make the bike lean as fast as possible, we want:

  • Reduced gyroscopic force. Two prominent sources: the wheels and the engine. Roll off the throttle to settle the engine, and brake hard to reduce the gyroscopic force of the wheels.
  • A steeper rake angle (the angle between the steering and the road). A steeper angle makes the wheel counter-steer faster — it's leverage. Moving a longer stick takes more force than a shorter one; pushing a door right next to its hinge is hard.
  • A shorter wheelbase. The same leverage idea, but lateral. Picture the bike as a stick connecting two contact patches — a longer stick needs more force to move.
Part III

The Path

Grip and inputs decide what the bike can do; the line decides where you do it. A good line straightens the corner — which means less lean for the same speed, and grip handed back for braking and drive.

Section 11

Race Lines

  • You want to straighten the turn as much as possible.
  • The apex is the point where you are closest to the inside of the turn.
  • On the road, stay to the outside until you can see the exit of the corner, then pick 2–3 points (apex, exit, and any others you need) to form the line to aim at.
Part IV

Putting It Together

Now overlay the inputs onto the line. A corner is one continuous handover: brakes to lean, lean to throttle — executed along the line you chose, never spending more grip than the tyre has.

Section 12

Cornering — Entry, Apex, Exit

Break a corner into three parts: entry, apex, exit.

  • Entry: throttle at 0%, braking at 100%.
  • Entry → apex: decrease braking, increase lean.
  • At the apex: approaching 100% lean and 0% braking. Right before you reach 0% braking, take the slack out of the throttle so there's never a frame where you're telling the bike to do nothing.
  • Apex → exit: decrease lean, increase throttle.
